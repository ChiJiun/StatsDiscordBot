### Problem Statement
Researchers designed a survey to compare the proportions of children who come to school without eating breakfast in two low-income elementary schools. An SRS of 80 students from school 1 found that 19 had not eaten breakfast. At School 2, an SRS of 150 students included 26 who had not eaten breakfast. More than 1500 students attend each school. Do these data give convincing evidence at the α = 0.05 level of a difference in the population proportions?

### Model Solution
State: Hypotheses and Parameters
We want to test if there is a significant difference in the proportion of students who skip breakfast between two low-income elementary schools at the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level.
Parameters:
$p_1$: The true proportion of students from School 1 who come to school without eating breakfast.
$p_2$: The true proportion of students from School 2 who come to school without eating breakfast.
Hypotheses:
$H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0$ (There is no difference).
$H_a: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0$ (There is a difference—two-sided test).
Significance Level: $\alpha = 0.05$.

Plan: Procedure and Conditions
Procedure: Two-sample $z$-test for $p_1 - p_2$.
To perform this test, we must verify the conditions for the sampling distribution of $\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2$:
1. Random:
School 1: SRS of 80 students.
School 2: SRS of 150 students.
Conclusion: Because both samples are random, $\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2$ is an unbiased estimator of $p_1 - p_2$.
2. Independent (10% Rule):
School 1: $80 \le 10\%$ of all students (Population > 800).
School 2: $150 \le 10\%$ of all students (Population > 1,500).
Conclusion: The samples are independent of each other and their respective populations, allowing us to use the standard error formula for the difference.
3. Normal (Large Counts):
Using the pooled proportion $\hat{p}_c \approx 0.196$:
School 1: $n_1\hat{p}_c = 15.68 \ge 10$ and $n_1(1-\hat{p}_c) = 64.32 \ge 10$.
School 2: $n_2\hat{p}_c = 29.4 \ge 10$ and $n_2(1-\hat{p}_c) = 120.6 \ge 10$.
Conclusion: Since the counts are large enough for both samples, the sampling distribution of $\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2$ is approximately Normal.

Do: Calculations
Sample Proportions:
$\hat{p}_1 = \frac{19}{80} = 0.2375$, $\hat{p}_2 = \frac{26}{150} \approx 0.1733$
Pooled Proportion ($\hat{p}_c$): $\frac{19+26}{80+150} \approx 0.1957$
Test Statistic ($z$):
$$z = \frac{(0.2375 - 0.1733) - 0}{\sqrt{0.1957(0.8043)(\frac{1}{80} + \frac{1}{150})}} \approx 1.168$$
P-value:
$$2 \times P(Z > 1.168) \approx 0.243$$

Conclude: Interpretation 
Statistical Decision: 
Comparison using P-value: Since the P-value (0.243) is greater than the significance level $\alpha = 0.05$, we fail to reject the null hypothesis ($H_0$).
Comparison using Test Statistic: Since the calculated test statistic $z \approx 1.168$ does not fall in the rejection region (defined by the critical values of $\pm 1.96$ for a two-tailed test at $\alpha = 0.05$), we fail to reject $H_0$.
Contextual Conclusion: 
We do not have convincing evidence that there is a difference in the true proportion of students who skip breakfast between School 1 and School 2.
